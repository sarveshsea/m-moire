# AICP Bidding Industry & Commercial Production Landscape

**Research Date:** March 24, 2026
**Status:** Comprehensive industry research covering bidding standards, market metrics, trends, and regulatory landscape

---

## Executive Summary

The commercial production bidding industry operates within a standardized framework established by AICP (Association of Independent Commercial Producers) since 1975. The US video/film production market is valued at **$40.9 billion (2025)** with a 2% growth rate. Key trends include AI-driven production cost reductions (30-60% savings), virtual production adoption, and increased focus on sustainability and DEI line items. The bidding process typically spans 10-16 weeks from RFP to award, with average win rates around 17-25%.

## 1. AICP Guidelines & Standards

### Bid Form Structure (2024-2025)

**Format & Organization:**
- **Letter-based sections:** A through X (expanded from original A-N)
- **Total line items:** 311-399+ line items depending on form version
- **Excel & PDF formats:** Available on AICP website

**Major Budget Sections (A-X):**

| Section | Category | Contents |
|---------|----------|----------|
| **A & C** | Pre-Production & Shooting Crew Labor | Casting, location scouting, pre-light, strike days, talent agency fees |
| **D** | Location & Travel Bid | Travel costs, accommodation, ground transport |
| **E** | Props, Wardrobe, Animal | Physical items, makeup, greens (rented plants/shrubs) |
| **F** | Soundstage Rental | Build days vs. shoot days, studio rental costs |
| **H** | Art Department Materials | Hardware, set dressing, meals |
| **I** | Equipment Rental | Camera packages, lighting, grip, sound, specialty rigs |
| **J** | Physical Media & Handling | Film stock, hard drives, memory cards |
| **K** | Miscellaneous Production | Catchall for items that don't fit elsewhere |
| **O & P** | Custom/Reassigned Sections | Production-specific additions, special categories |
| **Q-W** | Post-Production (Expanded) | Editorial, VFX, color, sound design, music licensing, social deliverables |

**Key Updates (Recent):**
- Sections O and P reassigned while remaining part of bidding structure
- Greatly expanded set of budget sections for post-production tasks
- Sections Q-W now incorporate former AICE (Association of Independent Commercial Editors) sections
- Adapted to accommodate social media deliverables

### AICP Markup Rates (2024 Standard)

- **Standard production company markup:** 25% (within industry standard)
- **Travel handling fee:** 15% (minimum when travel requirements are simple)
- **Annual wage bumps:** Built into 2024 structure (not single flat rate for contract term)

## 4. Bidding Process & Workflow

### Standard RFP-to-Award Pipeline (5 Stages)

**Stage 1: Planning & Preparation (Weeks 1-2)**
- Internal stakeholder alignment
- Budget category identification
- Market exploration & supplier conversations
- Spend analysis for efficiency opportunities

**Stage 2: RFP Development (Weeks 2-4)**
- Detailed RFP document creation
- Project scope definition
- Vendor expectation clarification
- Evaluation criteria establishment

**Stage 3: Distribution (Weeks 4-6)**
- RFP sent to potential bidders
- Public posting (if applicable)
- Bidder Q&A period
- Clarification responses

**Stage 4: Evaluation (Weeks 6-14)**
- Proposal review & scoring
- Commercial terms evaluation
- Shortlisting of viable bids
- Follow-up questions with bidders
- Use of evaluation matrices (scoring-based decisions)

**Stage 5: Selection & Award (Weeks 14-16)**
- Finalist negotiation
- Terms finalization
- Award letter & purchase order (PO) or letter of understanding (LOU)
- Formal contract execution
- Project kickoff

**Average Timeline:** 10-16 weeks total

**Source:** [Sievo - How to Run an RFP Process](https://sievo.com/blog/rfp-process), [RFQ Pro - 5 Steps from RFP to Award](https://www.rfqpro.com/rfp-request-for-proposal/from-rfp-to-award/), [Loopio - 5 Steps to Build a Simple RFP Process](https://loopio.com/blog/rfp-process-steps/)

## 7. SAG-AFTRA Rate Changes & Union Negotiations

### 2024 Low Budget Digital Waiver (Effective June 27, 2024 - March 31, 2025)

**Key Changes:**

**Unified Rate Structure:**
- Connected and Unconnected shoots now have parity
- Session and overtime rates no longer freely negotiable
- All rates must follow 2022 SAG-AFTRA Commercials Contract minimums

**Principal & Extra Performer Requirements:**
- Minimum scale session rates (non-negotiable)
- Mandatory overtime fees per contract

**Maximum Period of Use (MPU) Extension:**
- **New Timeline:** Later of (1) one year from first use OR (2) 13 weeks after last production date
- **Previous Timeline:** More restrictive terms
- **Impact:** More flexible timing for content release and distribution

**IATSE-AICP Commercial Rates (2024-2025):**
- Annual wage bumps integrated (not single flat rate)
- New Commercial Production Agreement from IATSE and AICP restructured rates
- Different rates by region and IATSE local (665, 798, 399, etc.)

**Budget Planning Implications:**
- Talent costs increasing annually via built-in escalators
- Less negotiating flexibility on day rates
- Digital waiver provides some relief for low-budget productions
- Must budget for union compliance across all talent categories

**Source:** [Wrapbook - Essential Guide to SAG-AFTRA Rates 2025](https://www.wrapbook.com/blog/essential-guide-sag-rates), [SAG-AFTRA - Rate Sheet Network TV Code 2024-2025](https://www.sagaftra.org/rate-sheet-sag-aftra-network-tv-code-2024-2025), [CMS Productions - 2025 SAG Agreements & Rates](https://cmsproductions.com/blog/sag-rates-agreements/)

## 9. Regulatory Landscape & Transparency

### Corporate Transparency Act (CTA) - 2024

**Effective Date:** January 1, 2024

**Scope:**
- Applies to more than 32 million U.S. businesses
- Requires beneficial ownership disclosure to FinCEN (Treasury Department)

**Requirements:**
- Identify all beneficial owners (those who own/control company)
- Report to U.S. Treasury Department's Financial Crimes Enforcement Network
- Regular updates on ownership changes

**Production Industry Impact:**
- Production companies must comply with CTA reporting
- Vendor transparency requirements increased
- Supply chain documentation for ownership & control

### Environmental & Supply Chain Transparency (2024)

**Scope 1, 2, 3 Emissions Reporting:**
- Carbon emissions disclosure requirements expanding
- Impact on production equipment, energy usage, travel
- Location scouting & transportation carbon accounting

**EU Battery Regulation & EU CBAM:**
- Affects equipment imports (backup generators, batteries, rigs)
- Chemical content & recycling requirements
- Carbon border adjustment applies to production equipment

**Chemical Substance Restrictions:**
- TSCA Section 6(h) restrictions on persistent, bioaccumulative, toxic chemicals
- Enforcement ramping up across industries
- Impacts paints, set materials, specialty effects

### AICP-Specific Regulatory Guidance

**Bidding Transparency:**
- AICP Bidding Guidelines emphasize transparent, competitive arena
- Best practices on realistic cost estimation
- Documentation of cost assumptions
- No bid-rigging or excessive budget padding

**Historical Note:** Industry moved past 1980s-90s bidding problems through standardization and ethics enforcement

## 12. DIBS (Digital Itemized Bid Summary) & Production Bidding Terminology

### What DIBS Means in Agency Production

"DIBS" — Digital Itemized Bid Summary — is not a recognized industry-standard acronym or product name in the advertising/media agency production ecosystem. Extensive web research across AICP resources, agency production forums, holding company documentation, and production software vendors returns no results for a tool, platform, or standard called "DIBS."

**What the industry actually uses:**

The standardized bidding format in commercial production is the **AICP Bid Form**, introduced in 1975 by the Association of Independent Commercial Producers. This form IS the "digital itemized bid summary" of the industry — it is the canonical line-item breakdown used by every major agency, production company, and cost consultant.

**Key clarification:** If "DIBS" is being used internally at a specific agency or holding company, it is a proprietary term, not an industry standard. The universal language is:
- **AICP Bid Form** — The standard line-item bid document (Excel/PDF)
- **Topsheet** — The summary page (page 1) of the AICP bid form showing rolled-up category totals
- **Cost Summary / Summary of Estimated Production Costs** — The formal name for the topsheet
- **Bid Package** — The complete submission (bid form + bid letter + treatment + calendar)
- **Triple Bid** — The process of soliciting 3+ competing bids from production companies

## 13. AICP Bid Form — Deep Structure

### Topsheet (Page 1 — Summary of Estimated Production Costs)

The topsheet is the 30,000-foot view. It rolls up every section into category-level totals. This is what the client and agency business affairs review first.

**Topsheet sections include:**
- Job information (client, product, agency, job number)
- Production information (director, production company, shoot dates, locations)
- Summary of all budget sections (A through X) with subtotals
- Above-the-line totals
- Below-the-line totals
- Production fee / markup (typically 25%)
- Insurance (typically 3% of A-K production costs)
- Travel handling fee (15% minimum)
- Grand total

### Section-by-Section Breakdown (A through X)

| Section | Category | ATL/BTL | Key Line Items |
|---------|----------|---------|----------------|
| **A** | Pre-Production & Wrap Labor | ATL | Casting director, location scout, production designer, pre-light, strike days |
| **B** | Shooting Crew Labor | BTL | Director of Photography, camera operators, gaffer, key grip, sound mixer, all day rates + OT |
| **C** | Pre-Production Expenses | BTL | Casting expenses, callbacks, studio rentals for casting |
| **D** | Location & Travel | BTL | Location fees, permits, travel days, hotels, per diems, ground transport, flights |
| **E** | Props, Wardrobe, Makeup & Animals | BTL | Wardrobe purchases/rentals, makeup supplies, hair, greens (rented plants), animal wranglers |
| **F** | Studio & Stage Rental | BTL | Build days, shoot days, strike days, studio rental, power, AC |
| **G** | Art Department Labor | BTL | Production designer, art director, set decorator, scenic artist, carpenters |
| **H** | Art Department Materials | BTL | Lumber, paint, hardware, set dressing, purchases, rentals |
| **I** | Equipment Rental | BTL | Camera packages, lighting, grip, sound, specialty rigs (cranes, Steadicam, drones) |
| **J** | Film & Digital Media | BTL | Hard drives, memory cards, data management, formerly film stock |
| **K** | Miscellaneous Production | BTL | Catchall — catering, craft services, parking, tips, contingency |
| **L** | Director / Creative Fees | ATL | Director's fee, creative/prep fees |
| **M** | Talent Labor | ATL | SAG-AFTRA principals, extras, session fees, fitting fees, travel days |
| **N** | Talent Expenses | ATL | Talent travel, wardrobe allowance, per diem for talent |
| **O** | Post-Production Labor (Reassigned) | BTL | Editor, assistant editor, colorist, sound mixer labor |
| **P** | Post-Production Expenses (Reassigned) | BTL | Edit suite rental, color session, mix stage |
| **Q-W** | Expanded Post-Production | BTL | VFX, animation, color grading, sound design, music licensing, social media deliverables, versioning |
| **X** | Custom / Additional | BTL | Production-specific additions that don't fit elsewhere |

### Above-the-Line vs. Below-the-Line

**Above-the-Line (ATL):**
- Director/creative fees (L)
- Talent labor and expenses (M, N)
- These are the "creative" costs — typically negotiated separately and not subject to the same markup structure

**Below-the-Line (BTL):**
- Everything else — crew, equipment, locations, art department, post-production
- These are the "production" costs — subject to standard markup (25%) and insurance (3%)

### Markup Structure

| Markup | Rate | Applied To |
|--------|------|------------|
| **Production Fee** | 25% (standard) | Below-the-line subtotal |
| **Insurance** | 3% | Sections A-K production costs |
| **Travel Handling** | 15% (minimum) | Travel-related costs when travel is simple |
| **Weather Day** | Varies | Budgeted as contingency for outdoor shoots |
| **Overtime** | 1.5x / 2x | Crew hourly rates after 10/12 hours |

## 14. The Triple Bid Process

### How It Works

1. **Agency receives approved creative** (storyboards, scripts) from client
2. **Agency producer identifies 3+ production companies** (and typically specific directors each represents)
3. **RFP/bid specs distributed** to all bidders simultaneously with creative brief, boards, and budget range
4. **Production companies submit bid packages** (AICP bid form + bid letter + director's treatment + proposed schedule)
5. **Agency producer reviews and compares** all bids side-by-side, section by section
6. **Agency recommends preferred company** to client based on creative approach, production scope, and price
7. **Client approves** (or requests revisions/re-bids)
8. **Award letter / PO / LOU issued** to winning bidder

### Industry Tensions

- **Holding company in-house production** (Hogarth/WPP, Omnicom Production) sometimes competes in triple bids against independent production companies, creating conflicts of interest
- **Cost consultants** increasingly mediate the process, adding a layer of scrutiny
- **Budget thresholds** determine whether triple bidding is mandatory — varies by agency/holding company policy and client contract
- **Bidders should not know who else is bidding** per cost consultant rules, though information leaks through industry networks
- **Production companies sink significant time and money** into bid preparation with no guarantee of award (17-25% win rates)

## 15. Agency Producer Workflow — Creating a Bid

### Step-by-Step Producer Workflow

**Phase 1: Brief & Scope (Day 1)**
1. Receive creative brief, storyboards, and client-approved concept
2. Identify director preferences and production company shortlist
3. Determine budget range and any client-mandated constraints
4. Distribute bid specs to 3+ production companies

**Phase 2: Bid Compilation (Days 2-4, typically 3 working days)**
1. Production company EP (Executive Producer) and line producer review boards
2. Director writes treatment (creative approach document)
3. Line producer begins populating AICP bid form:
   - Start with crew rates (Section B) — largest variable
   - Build out equipment needs (Section I) based on director's vision
   - Estimate locations (Section D) and art department (G, H)
   - Calculate talent costs (M, N) per SAG-AFTRA rates
   - Spec out post-production (O-W)
   - Add markup, insurance, contingency
4. EP reviews and adjusts to hit target budget range
5. Bid letter drafted explaining creative approach and any assumptions
6. Complete bid package assembled (topsheet + detailed breakdown + letter + treatment + calendar)

**Phase 3: Submission & Review (Days 4-7)**
1. Bid packages submitted to agency producer
2. Agency producer reviews all 3+ bids side-by-side
3. Compares section-by-section: Where are the differences? Why?
4. Identifies cost discrepancies and requests clarifications
5. May request revisions or re-bids on specific sections

**Phase 4: Client Presentation (Days 7-10)**
1. Agency presents recommended production company to client
2. Client reviews topsheet summaries and creative treatments
3. Client may request changes or additional bids
4. Final approval and award

### Common Pain Points in This Workflow

| Pain Point | Description | Impact |
|------------|-------------|--------|
| **Manual Excel entry** | 311-399+ line items populated by hand in Excel or Excel-based tools | Error-prone, slow, formula breakage |
| **Version chaos** | Multiple bid revisions emailed as file attachments (v1, v2, v2_final, v2_final_FINAL) | Lost changes, wrong version submitted |
| **No real-time collaboration** | Hot Budget is single-user, desktop-only; traditional AICP Excel form has no collaboration | EP and line producer can't work simultaneously |
| **Section navigation** | Scrolling through 300+ line items in a single Excel workbook with multiple tabs | Cognitive overload, easy to miss sections |
| **Rate lookups** | SAG-AFTRA rates, IATSE rates, equipment rental rates must be manually referenced from external sources | Outdated rates, calculation errors |
| **Comparison difficulty** | Agency producer must manually compare 3+ bid forms section-by-section | Time-consuming, inconsistent comparison criteria |
| **Markup calculations** | Production fee (25%), insurance (3%), travel handling (15%) must be manually calculated and applied | Math errors in high-stakes documents |
| **Revision tracking** | No built-in change tracking — changes between bid versions require manual comparison | Client/agency may not see what changed |
| **Format requirements** | Client/agency may require specific output format (Hot Budget topsheet, AICP PDF) even if built in different tool | Export/reformatting friction |
| **Institutional knowledge** | Cost estimation relies heavily on producer experience — no systematic rate databases | Inconsistent pricing, junior producer struggles |
| **Client-facing vs. internal** | Different level of detail shown to client (topsheet only) vs. internal team (full breakdown) | Maintaining two views of same data |
| **Multi-format deliverables** | Social, broadcast, digital may each need separate bids or bid versions | Multiplied effort for multi-platform campaigns |

## 18. WCAG 2.1 AA Accessibility for Complex Bidding Interfaces

### Critical Requirements for a Bid Management Tool

A bidding/estimating tool with 300+ line items across multiple sections presents significant accessibility challenges. The following WCAG 2.1 AA requirements are essential:

### 18.1 Data Table Accessibility (WCAG 1.3.1 — Info and Relationships)

**Semantic Structure:**
- Use native HTML `<table>`, `<thead>`, `<tbody>`, `<th>`, `<td>` elements
- Associate header cells with data cells using `scope` or `headers` attributes
- For complex multi-level headers (section > subsection > line item), use explicit `id` and `headers` attributes
- Avoid using `<div>` grids that mimic table layout without semantic meaning

**ARIA Grid Pattern (for interactive/editable cells):**
- Apply `role="grid"` to the table container
- Use `role="row"`, `role="columnheader"`, `role="gridcell"` appropriately
- Implement `aria-sort` on sortable column headers
- Use `aria-readonly`, `aria-selected`, `aria-expanded` for cell states
- Announce changes to screen readers via `aria-live` regions

### 18.2 Keyboard Navigation (WCAG 2.1.1 — Keyboard)

**Data Grid Navigation:**
- Arrow keys to navigate between cells (up/down/left/right)
- Tab to move between interactive widgets within cells
- Enter to begin editing a cell; Escape to cancel edit
- Home/End to jump to first/last cell in a row
- Ctrl+Home/Ctrl+End to jump to first/last cell in the grid
- Page Up/Page Down to scroll by viewport height

**Section Navigation:**
- Provide keyboard shortcuts to jump between major sections (A through X)
- Collapsible sections must be operable via Enter/Space
- Focus must move logically when sections expand/collapse

**Focus Management:**
- Only one cell in the grid should be in the tab sequence at a time (roving tabindex)
- Visible focus indicator with minimum 3:1 contrast ratio
- Focus must not become trapped in any section or modal

### 18.3 Color and Contrast (WCAG 1.4.3, 1.4.11)

- Text contrast minimum 4.5:1 against background
- Large text (18px+ or 14px+ bold) minimum 3:1
- Non-text UI components (cell borders, buttons, focus indicators) minimum 3:1
- Do not rely solely on color to convey information (e.g., red for over-budget requires additional indicator like icon or text)

### 18.4 Form Inputs and Labels (WCAG 1.3.1, 3.3.2)

- Every editable cell must have an accessible label (column header + row context)
- Error messages must be programmatically associated with the field (`aria-describedby`)
- Required fields must be indicated both visually and programmatically (`aria-required`)
- Input purpose must be clear — currency fields should indicate currency, rate fields should indicate unit (per day, per hour)

### 18.5 Responsive Reflow (WCAG 1.4.10 — Reflow)

- Content must reflow at 320px CSS width without horizontal scrolling for single-column content
- For data-dense tables, this may require:
  - Card-based layout at small viewports
  - Horizontal scroll with sticky first column (acceptable per WCAG for data tables)
  - Collapsible columns to reduce horizontal extent

### 18.6 Error Prevention (WCAG 3.3.4 — Error Prevention for Legal/Financial)

Since bid forms involve financial data:
- Submissions must be reversible, checked, or confirmed
- Users must be able to review and correct data before final submission
- Auto-save should be implemented to prevent data loss
- Undo/redo support for cell edits

### 18.7 Text Spacing and Zoom (WCAG 1.4.12, 1.4.4)

- Content must remain functional at 200% zoom
- Text spacing adjustments (line-height 1.5x, paragraph spacing 2x, letter spacing 0.12em, word spacing 0.16em) must not break layout
- Numbers in financial cells must remain readable at all supported zoom levels

## 19. UX Patterns for Bidding/Estimating Workflows

### 19.1 Progressive Disclosure for 300+ Line Items

**Section-Based Accordion Pattern:**
- Group line items into collapsible sections (A through X)
- Show section-level subtotals when collapsed
- Expand individual sections for detail editing
- Allow multi-section expansion for cross-referencing

**Three-Level Navigation:**
1. **Topsheet view** — Category totals only (what the client sees)
2. **Section view** — Line items within one section
3. **Detail view** — Individual line item with all fields (quantity, rate, unit, days, total, notes)

### 19.2 Sticky Context

- **Sticky column headers** — Always visible when scrolling vertically
- **Sticky row identifiers** — Line item names visible when scrolling horizontally
- **Sticky topsheet summary** — Running total visible at all times (footer or sidebar)
- **Sticky section header** — Current section name visible when deep in a section

### 19.3 Inline Editing

- Click/tap to edit individual cells (spreadsheet-like interaction)
- Tab to advance to next editable cell
- Automatic calculation propagation (change a rate, total updates instantly)
- Visual indicators for calculated vs. manually entered values
- Cell-level validation (negative numbers, unreasonable values, rate range warnings)

### 19.4 Smart Defaults and Auto-Fill

- **Rate databases** — Pre-populated crew rates from SAG-AFTRA, IATSE schedules
- **Equipment packages** — Standard camera/lighting/grip packages as templates
- **Historical data** — "Similar to your last bid for [client]" suggestions
- **Market benchmarks** — Flag when a line item is significantly above/below market average

### 19.5 Multi-Bid Comparison View

- **Side-by-side columns** — Show 3+ bids with shared row structure
- **Difference highlighting** — Color-code cells where bids diverge significantly
- **Variance analysis** — Show percentage and dollar differences per section
- **Recommendation annotations** — Agency producer can add notes per section

### 19.6 Version Control and Revision Tracking

- **Named versions** — "Initial Bid," "Rev 1 — Client Feedback," "Rev 2 — Final"
- **Change log** — What changed between versions (line item, old value, new value, who changed it, when)
- **Diff view** — Visual comparison between any two versions
- **Restore** — Ability to revert to any previous version

### 19.7 Role-Based Views

| Role | View | Capabilities |
|------|------|-------------|
| **Line Producer** | Full detail — all sections, all line items | Create, edit, all fields |
| **Executive Producer** | Full detail with margin visibility | Edit, approve, set markup |
| **Agency Producer** | Topsheet + section summaries + comparison | Review, comment, request changes |
| **Client / Business Affairs** | Topsheet summary only | Review, approve/reject |
| **Cost Consultant** | Full detail + benchmark comparison | Review, flag anomalies, audit |

### 19.8 Data Density Management

- **Compact mode** — Maximum data density for experienced producers (small type, tight spacing)
- **Standard mode** — Balanced readability and density
- **Presentation mode** — Clean, spacious layout for client reviews
- **Print/PDF mode** — Formatted for standard AICP output

### 19.9 Contextual Help and Validation

- Tooltip explanations for each AICP section and line item
- Real-time validation (e.g., "OT rate should be 1.5x base rate")
- Rate card reference panel — SAG-AFTRA, IATSE rates visible alongside bid
- Budget alerts — "You are 15% over the agency's stated budget range"

## 21. Bid Revision and Version Control Workflows

### Current State (How Agencies Handle It Today)

**The Email Chain Problem:**
1. Line producer creates bid in Hot Budget or Excel
2. Saves as `ProjectName_Bid_v1.xlsx`
3. Emails to EP for review
4. EP makes changes, saves as `ProjectName_Bid_v1_EP.xlsx`
5. EP sends to agency producer
6. Agency producer requests changes, emails back
7. Line producer revises, saves as `ProjectName_Bid_v2.xlsx`
8. Client requests changes after agency presents
9. Line producer creates `ProjectName_Bid_v2_revised.xlsx`
10. Repeat until award or loss

**Problems:**
- No single source of truth
- Changes not tracked granularly
- Easy to submit wrong version
- No audit trail for compliance
- Comparing versions requires manual side-by-side review

### Client-Agency Comparison/Review Workflow

**How the comparison typically works:**

1. **Agency producer receives 3 bid packages** (PDF/Excel)
2. **Creates a comparison document** — Often a separate Excel workbook with columns for each bidder
3. **Manually transposes key numbers** from each bid into comparison columns
4. **Highlights major variances** — Where do the bids diverge? Why?
5. **Writes recommendation memo** — Which company and why
6. **Presents to client** — Usually topsheet-level summary with recommendation
7. **Client reviews** — May ask for specific line items to be revised
8. **Revisions cycle** — Back to step 1 with updated bids

**What would improve this:**
- Automated bid ingestion and comparison
- Real-time collaboration between agency and production company on bid adjustments
- Structured change tracking between revisions
- Client portal for review and approval with annotation capabilities
- Audit trail for compliance and cost consultant review

## 22. Bid Comparison and Review Best Practices

### Current Tools Used for Review/Approval

| Tool | Used For | Limitation for Bid Review |
|------|----------|--------------------------|
| **Email + PDF** | Sending bid packages | No structured comparison, no version tracking |
| **Excel side-by-side** | Manual comparison | Error-prone manual data entry, no collaboration |
| **Filestage** | Creative asset review | Not designed for financial/tabular data |
| **Frame.io** | Video review | Not applicable to bid forms |
| **Google Sheets** | Shared spreadsheets | Not AICP-formatted, no bid-specific features |
| **BriefBid** | RFP/triple bid management | Manages process, limited bid content comparison |

### Ideal Review Workflow

1. **Structured ingestion** — Upload or import bid forms from any format (Hot Budget, Saturation, Excel, PDF)
2. **Normalized comparison** — Map all bids to common line-item structure regardless of source format
3. **Variance dashboard** — Visual heatmap showing where bids differ by >10%, >25%, >50%
4. **Section drill-down** — Click any section to see line-item detail across all bidders
5. **Annotation layer** — Agency producer and client can add comments per line item
6. **Approval workflow** — Section-by-section or topsheet-level approve/reject with rationale
7. **Revision request** — Structured feedback sent back to production company with specific change requests
8. **Audit trail** — Every action logged for compliance
